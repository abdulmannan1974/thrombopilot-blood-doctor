# Thrombophilia Testing Algorithm

**Type**: Interactive Algorithm
**Scraped**: 2026-03-12

---

## Overview

Interactive decision-support algorithm guiding clinicians on when and what thrombophilia tests to order in patients with venous thromboembolism (VTE).

---

## Step 1: Is this the patient's FIRST VTE?

- **Yes** → proceed to Step 2
- **No** (recurrent VTE) → Thrombophilia testing generally NOT indicated (patient already qualifies for long-term anticoagulation)

---

## Step 2: VTE Location

### Usual Site
- Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) of the arm or leg
- Pulmonary embolism (PE)

### Unusual Site
- Intracranial (cerebral venous sinus thrombosis)
- Abdominal (portal, mesenteric, hepatic, splenic vein)
- Other unusual location

---

## Step 3: Provoking Risk Factors

### Major (Transient) Risk Factor
- Surgery with general anesthesia > 30 minutes
- Confined to bed in hospital ≥ 3 days
- Caesarean section
- Major trauma / fracture

**If major transient risk factor present → Thrombophilia testing generally NOT recommended**

### Minor (Transient) Risk Factor
- Estrogen therapy (OCP, HRT)
- Pregnancy/postpartum
- Leg injury with reduced mobility
- Confined to bed out of hospital ≥ 3 days
- Long-haul flight (> 8 hours)

### No Identifiable Risk Factor (Unprovoked)
→ Consider thrombophilia testing in select situations

---

## When to Test for Thrombophilia

Testing may be considered in at least ONE of the following situations:

1. **First unprovoked VTE** at age < 50 AND results would change management (e.g., duration of anticoagulation, family screening)
2. **Unprovoked VTE at unusual site** (cerebral, splanchnic)
3. **VTE in the setting of a weak provoking factor** AND young age
4. **Family history** of VTE in first-degree relative, especially if young
5. **Pregnancy-related VTE** planning or future pregnancy planning
6. **Results would change management** for the individual patient

---

## When NOT to Test

- Recurrent VTE (already on long-term anticoagulation)
- VTE provoked by a major transient risk factor
- Acute phase of VTE (many tests unreliable during acute thrombosis/on anticoagulation)
- Elderly patients with multiple comorbidities (unlikely to change management)
- Routine screening of asymptomatic individuals

## Thrombophilia Panel (When Testing IS Indicated)

### Hereditary Thrombophilias

| Test | Prevalence (General Population) | VTE Risk (Relative) |
|------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Factor V Leiden (FVL) | 5% (heterozygous) | 3–8× (het), 50–80× (hom) |
| Prothrombin G20210A | 2–3% | 2–3× (het) |
| Antithrombin deficiency | 0.02–0.2% | 10–50× |
| Protein C deficiency | 0.2–0.5% | 7–10× |
| Protein S deficiency | 0.1–0.7% | 5–10× |

### Acquired Thrombophilias

| Test | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Antiphospholipid antibodies | Lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, anti-β2 glycoprotein I |

---

## Testing Considerations

### Timing
- **Do NOT test during acute VTE** — protein C, S, antithrombin affected
- **Do NOT test while on anticoagulation**:
  - Warfarin: affects protein C, protein S, lupus anticoagulant
  - Heparin: affects antithrombin, lupus anticoagulant
  - DOACs: affect lupus anticoagulant, may affect other clot-based assays
- **Best timing**: At least 2 weeks off anticoagulation (if safe to do so), and > 3 months after acute VTE

### Genetic Tests (unaffected by anticoagulation)
- Factor V Leiden mutation
- Prothrombin G20210A mutation
